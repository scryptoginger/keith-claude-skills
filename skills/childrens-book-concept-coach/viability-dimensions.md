# Viability Dimensions — Reference

This is the framework for honestly evaluating whether a children's book concept works. Use these dimensions to guide the conversation, but don't march through them as a checklist — start with whichever feels weakest based on what the author has shared, and use Socratic questions to let the author see gaps themselves where possible.

For each dimension below, you'll find:
- **What it is**
- **Diagnostic questions** to ask the author (or ask yourself about the concept)
- **Red flags** that signal the dimension isn't working
- **How to phrase honest pushback** when needed

---

## 1. Character

**What it is:** A protagonist a kid will want to spend time with. Not necessarily likable in a bland way — distinctive, specific, with a recognizable inner life.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Who is the main character? Describe them in three specific traits, not generic ones ("brave" doesn't count; "the kind of brave where she does the scary thing first then cries about it after" does).
- What does this character care about? What annoys them? What makes them laugh?
- What's the one thing about this character that no other character in any other book has?

**Red flags:**
- The character is defined entirely by their species, job, or premise ("a brave little fire truck", "a curious cat") with no specificity beneath that
- The author can't answer questions about what the character likes/dislikes/fears
- The character could be swapped for any other character without changing the story

**Honest pushback example:**
> "Right now your protagonist feels more like a placeholder than a person. 'A shy fox' is a starting point but it's not a character yet — there are dozens of shy-fox picture books. What makes this fox specifically? What's their voice? What do they sound like when they're excited or scared?"

---

## 2. Want and obstacle

**What it is:** The engine of every story. The protagonist wants something concrete; something is in the way; the tension between those drives the narrative.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What does the protagonist want? (Must be concrete and observable, not abstract. "To be happy" is not a want; "to find his missing hat" is.)
- Why can't they just have it?
- Are they actively trying to get it, or are they just experiencing things?

**Red flags:**
- The author describes a situation, character, or world but no want
- The "want" is internal and abstract (self-acceptance, confidence) without a concrete external goal that mirrors it
- The protagonist is reactive — things happen to them, they don't drive the action
- The obstacle is too easily resolved or doesn't actually matter

**Honest pushback example:**
> "I want to slow down here. You've described a beautiful character and a charming world, but I'm not hearing what your character is trying to do. Without a want, there's no engine. What does she actively go after in this story? And what's stopping her from getting it on page one?"

---

## 3. Stakes

**What it is:** Why it matters that the character gets (or doesn't get) what they want. Stakes don't have to be life-or-death — they have to feel real at the kid's emotional level.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What happens if the character fails? Why does the kid reading this care?
- Does the want connect to something the kid would recognize from their own emotional life?

**Red flags:**
- The author can't articulate why anything matters
- The stakes are adult stakes (career, money, romance) that don't translate
- The stakes are so artificial they feel manufactured ("if she doesn't water the magic flower, the whole kingdom dies" — okay, but does the kid actually feel that?)

**Honest pushback example:**
> "I get the plot but I don't feel the stakes. For a five-year-old reader, why does it matter whether he gets to school on time? Connect this to something a kid actually feels — the fear of being the only one who's late, the embarrassment of walking in with everyone watching. Where's the felt experience underneath the plot?"

---

## 4. Age-appropriateness

**What it is:** The match between the concept's emotional content, vocabulary, themes, and cognitive complexity and the target reader's developmental stage.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What ages is this for? (If unsure, work back from the concept.)
- Does the emotional content match what a kid that age can process and find satisfying?
- Are the themes ones a kid that age would actually care about, or ones an adult thinks they should care about?

**Red flags:**
- Adult themes pretending to be kid themes (existential dread dressed as a bedtime story)
- "Issue books" where the agenda overrides the story
- Vocabulary or sentence complexity wildly off-target for the format
- Concepts that are technically appropriate but emotionally inert for the age (e.g., a six-year-old doesn't care about the protagonist's mortgage problem, even if it's the parent's mortgage)

**Honest pushback example:**
> "Here's something I want to put on the table: the themes you're describing — grief, mortality, the way time changes love — are profound, but they're sitting in a picture book frame for ages 3–7. Picture books *can* handle hard themes, but only when filtered through a kid's emotional experience. Right now this reads like a book for adults *about* a kid. Who's your reader, really?"

---

## 5. Theme / "so what"

**What it is:** The emotional or thematic resonance underneath the plot. Not a moral lesson — a felt truth. The thing the book is *really* about, even if it never says so out loud.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- If a kid finishes this book and you asked them what it was about, what would they say? What would you *want* them to say?
- What emotional experience does the book deliver?
- Strip the plot away — what's left?

**Red flags:**
- The author leads with a moral ("It teaches kids that sharing is good")
- There's nothing underneath the surface story
- The theme is preachy or didactic
- The author can't answer "what is this book really about?"

**Honest pushback example:**
> "What's underneath this story? Right now I see a fun premise but I don't feel a heartbeat. The picture books that endure — *The Velveteen Rabbit*, *Last Stop on Market Street*, *Owl Moon* — all have something they're really about underneath the plot. Not a moral, just a felt truth. What's yours?"

**Important:** Theme and moral are different. Theme is *about* something; a moral *tells* the reader something. Modern children's literature, especially in traditional publishing, rejects moralizing. The book can have a clear theme without telling the reader what to think.

---

## 6. The re-read test

**What it is:** Picture books in particular get read 50+ times by tired parents. The book has to reward repeat reading — for both the kid (who wants the comfort of the familiar) and the adult (who needs something to hold their attention).

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Why would a parent want to read this 50 times without losing their mind?
- Why would a kid demand it again?
- Is there layered humor, lyrical language, surprising images, or rhythmic pleasure that holds up?

**Red flags:**
- The book depends entirely on plot surprise (won't survive the second reading)
- The language is functional but not pleasurable
- There's nothing for the adult — it's all kid-pitched
- The hook is novel but shallow

**Honest pushback example:**
> "Here's my re-read test concern: the joke at the heart of this is great the first time, but I'm not sure what's there for read number 20. Picture books live or die on re-readability. What in your book rewards a kid asking for it every night for a month? What gets richer or funnier each time?"

---

## 7. Hook / pitchability

**What it is:** Can the book be described in a single sentence that makes someone want to read it? This isn't just a marketing concern — if there's no clean hook, there's usually no clean concept.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Pitch the book in one sentence. What's the hook?
- If you said this sentence to a stranger at a party, would they say "oh, I want to read that to my kid"?
- Can you describe what's distinctive about it without using the word "heartwarming"?

**Red flags:**
- The author needs a paragraph to explain the concept
- The pitch sounds like every other book in the space
- The pitch is all setup with no twist or angle
- The author leans on "but it's really about..." to make it interesting (suggests the surface concept isn't doing the work)

**Honest pushback example:**
> "Try pitching this in one sentence. ... Okay, that took two minutes and three caveats. That's a sign the concept isn't tight yet. The strong picture book pitches sound like: 'A pigeon really, really wants to drive the bus.' One sentence, total clarity, immediate hook. What's your one-sentence version?"

---

## 8. Visual potential (picture books and illustrated formats)

**What it is:** Whether the concept gives an illustrator enough to work with — visual variety, interesting locations, expressive moments, opportunities for both quiet and loud spreads.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Walk through the book in your head, spread by spread. What's on each page?
- Is there variety, or are 14 spreads all the same character in the same room?
- Are there opportunities for visual humor, drama, scale changes?

**Red flags:**
- The whole book happens in one location with one character
- The action is all internal (thinking, feeling) with nothing to show
- The story depends on text the illustrator can't visualize
- Conversely: the visuals do all the work and the text is redundant

**Honest pushback example:**
> "I want to think about this as a 32-page object. Right now I'm seeing a lot of one character sitting and thinking. What does an illustrator do with that for 14 spreads? Picture books need visual energy and variety. Where can we open this up — different locations, different scales, moments that beg to be drawn?"

---

## 9. Voice

**What it is:** The distinctive sensibility and language of the book. Voice is what makes a book feel like only this author could have written it.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Read me the first line you've imagined, even if you haven't written anything yet — what does the book sound like?
- Is the voice doing anything distinctive, or is it generic "children's book voice"?
- Whose books does this remind you of? (And: is it because you love them, or because you're echoing them?)

**Red flags:**
- The author can't describe the voice at all
- The voice is generic warm/whimsical with no specificity
- The voice is borrowed wholesale from a famous author without transformation
- The voice doesn't match the concept (e.g., a sly Klassen-style premise told in earnest Mister Rogers cadence)

**Honest pushback example:**
> "I notice we've talked a lot about *what* the book is about but not about how it *sounds*. Voice is what separates the picture books that get remembered from the ones that get returned to the library and forgotten. Is the voice playful and rhythmic? Dry and deadpan? Lyrical and slow? What does this book sound like out loud?"

---

## How to use these dimensions in the conversation

Don't open with "Let me evaluate your idea on nine dimensions." That kills the conversation. Instead:

1. Listen first. Get the seed of the idea, the author's context, the format.
2. As they talk, mentally check which dimensions feel weakest.
3. Probe the weakest one first, Socratically when possible — ask a question that helps them see the gap themselves.
4. If they can answer well, move on. If they can't, that's where the work is.
5. By the end of the conversation, you should have a clear-eyed read on which dimensions are strong, which need work, and whether the concept is fundamentally viable.

The goal isn't to evaluate — it's to help the author *see clearly*. A skill that produces clarity, even uncomfortable clarity, is doing its job.
