# Children's Book Formats — Reference

Use this reference when helping the author identify which format their idea fits, or when flagging that their idea doesn't naturally fit any single format.

The format determines almost everything else: word count, structural conventions, pacing, vocabulary, what makes the concept viable, and what the author needs to deliver. Getting format wrong is the most common reason early-stage children's book ideas stall.

## Board books (ages 0–3)

- **Word count:** Often under 100 words, sometimes just labels
- **Page count:** 12–24 pages, very thick board pages
- **Audience:** Babies and toddlers, read by a parent
- **Concept demands:**
  - Single, simple concept (colors, opposites, animal sounds, bedtime, body parts)
  - High repetition and rhythm
  - Sturdy emotional anchor (love, comfort, recognition) — not narrative arc
  - Often interactive (touch-and-feel, lift-the-flap)
- **Red flags:** Anything with plot complexity, character development, or thematic nuance is wrong format. Move it up to picture book.
- **Examples:** *Goodnight Moon* (technically picture book but functions as board), *Brown Bear, Brown Bear, What Do You See?*, Sandra Boynton's catalog

## Picture books (ages 3–7, sometimes 4–8)

- **Word count:** 200–800 words is the modern sweet spot; under 500 is increasingly preferred
- **Page count:** Almost always exactly 32 pages (industry standard, dictated by printing)
- **Audience:** Read aloud by an adult to a child, or shared together
- **Concept demands:**
  - One clear protagonist with a clear want
  - A problem and a satisfying resolution
  - Strong visual potential — every spread needs something to show
  - Voice that holds up to repeat reading (parents read these dozens of times)
  - Page-turn momentum — each spread should make the reader want to turn the page
  - Often built around a single emotional or conceptual hook
- **Pacing convention:** 12–14 spreads of story, often with a "page-turn surprise" structure
- **Red flags:**
  - Word counts over 1,000 in 2026 are very hard to sell traditionally
  - Concepts that "tell" emotion instead of "showing" through action
  - Premises where the protagonist is passive (things happen to them rather than them driving)
  - Heavy moral lessons — kids and gatekeepers both reject these
- **Examples:** *Where the Wild Things Are*, *The Day the Crayons Quit*, *We Are in a Book!* (Mo Willems), *I Want My Hat Back* (Jon Klassen), *The Wonderful Things You Will Be*

## Early readers / Easy readers (ages 5–7)

- **Word count:** 1,500–3,500 words, broken into chapters or sections
- **Page count:** Usually 32–64 pages with simple illustrations on most pages
- **Audience:** Kids learning to read independently
- **Concept demands:**
  - Vocabulary controlled to early-reading level (varies by leveling system — Guided Reading, Lexile, Scholastic)
  - Short sentences, lots of repetition of high-frequency words
  - Plot simple enough to follow but engaging enough to motivate the work of reading
  - Often series-friendly (recurring characters, episodic adventures)
- **Red flags:** Sophisticated vocabulary, abstract themes, complex plots
- **Examples:** *Frog and Toad*, *Elephant and Piggie*, *Henry and Mudge*, *Mercy Watson*

## Chapter books (ages 6–9, sometimes 7–10)

- **Word count:** 5,000–15,000 words, divided into short chapters
- **Page count:** 60–120 pages, illustrations every few pages
- **Audience:** Newly independent readers building stamina
- **Concept demands:**
  - A strong, distinctive protagonist a kid wants to spend time with
  - Episodic-friendly structure (often series)
  - Humor or strong hook — kids choose these themselves
  - Real but not overwhelming stakes
  - Themes appropriate to elementary-school emotional life (friendship, family, school, fairness, fitting in)
- **Examples:** *Junie B. Jones*, *Magic Tree House*, *The Princess in Black*, *Stink*

## Middle grade (ages 8–12)

- **Word count:** 25,000–50,000 words for contemporary; up to 75,000 for fantasy
- **Page count:** 150–400 pages, mostly text, occasional illustrations
- **Audience:** Confident independent readers, often the peak reading-for-pleasure years
- **Concept demands:**
  - A protagonist roughly the age of the upper end of the target range, or slightly older
  - Genuine emotional depth — middle grade kids are sophisticated readers and will reject anything that talks down to them
  - Real stakes (death, divorce, identity, belonging are all on the table)
  - No on-page romance beyond crushes; no graphic violence; no profanity
  - Hope is essential — middle grade earns its hard moments by ultimately offering hope
- **Examples:** *Charlotte's Web*, *Bridge to Terabithia*, *The One and Only Ivan*, *When You Reach Me*, *Wonder*, *The Tale of Despereaux*

## Young adult (ages 12+)

If the author lands here, gently note that YA is its own world with very different conventions — and the skill's framing was built around children's books. Offer to continue the conversation but flag that the author may want a YA-specific resource.

## How to use this in conversation

When the author describes their idea, identify the format that fits and check it against the concept's demands. Common mismatches:

- "I want to write a picture book that teaches kids about [complex topic]" → Often the topic is wrong for picture book format. Either simplify radically or move up.
- "It's a long story about a complicated friendship" for ages 4–7 → Probably middle grade in disguise.
- "It's a quick fun book about [single concept]" for ages 8–10 → Probably belongs in picture book or early reader, not chapter book.

When you spot a mismatch, name it directly:

> "I want to flag something: the emotional content you're describing — grief, complicated parent relationships, identity questions — that's middle-grade territory. As a picture book, you'd have to simplify it so much that you'd lose what makes it interesting. Want to talk about reframing this for an older reader?"
